I Had My First Technical Interview (sort of)

This brief article first appeared on my LinkedIn page.

A few days ago a fellow student in Udacity's Grow with Google Scholarship program told me about a site called Pramp. As their "About Us" page will tell you, "Pramp pairs software developers together for live practice interviews based on their professional background, availability and practicing needs."

I thought I'd give it a shot and let you know how it went.

After signing up and filling out a short questionnaire about my area of study (frontend) and skill level (beginner), I made an appointment for an available slot in their calendar. A little while later, I received an email telling me that Pramp had paired me with someone.

The interview took place a couple of days later via video chat. My paired partner and I used Pramp's realtime collaborative code editor to watch each other work. Pretty cool. Pramp had provided each of us a problem to present when it was our turn to play interviewer. We were also given hints and a complete solution to the problem. That way, the interviewer could provide the interviewee a bit of coaching when they got stuck.

Neither of us had been in a actual technical interview, and we didn't have much idea what we were doing. I was a bit nervous and asked if I could take on the interviewer role first. The problem was pretty tough. My partner had some good ideas, but wasn't quite able to get to a solution. I'd spent an hour the night before going over the problem and I hadn't been able to solve it either.

I went next. By this point, I'd gotten over most of my jitters. My problem wasn't as tough as my partners, but I still wasn't able to get to the preferred solution. My partner did his best to coach me, but I just didn't have the background necessary. I could easily provide the brute force solution, but wasn't able to implement the algorithm that would have satisfied the problem's constraints. Good to know! I guess I need to work on algorithms.

The interview was about an hour long. Once it was over, Pramp popped up a questionnaire and we each provided detailed feedback about the other's interview performance and about interviewing style.

What I found most valuable about this experience was being put on the spot and forced to perform in front of one of my peers. It was just as nerve-wracking as it sounds. Which is sort of the point. Code Wars, Leet Code, Hacker Rank, etc. are great, and they'll help you learn what you need to learn to solve the kinds of problems you'll encounter in a real interview. What they won't do is prepare you for the experience of being interviewed. There's nothing quite like it: the butterflies, the clammy hands, the "oh, shit" realization that you're about to judged by someone you just met on your prowess as a programmer. It takes time and practice to become accustomed to those feelings and to gain the confidence that you can perform. Pramp provides a means to do that.

I'll be using Pramp again, and frequently.
